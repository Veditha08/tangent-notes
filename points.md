4. The Problem Nobody Talks About

Large Language Models have dramatically improved reasoning.

Their interfaces haven't.

Today's leading AI products still rely on a design paradigm borrowed from messaging apps:

A chronological conversation.

That paradigm works for conversations.

It begins to fail when AI becomes a thinking partner.

Already, this sounds much stronger than

"Existing LLMs are linear."

Because you're explaining why they're linear.

5. Conversations are linear.

Thinking isn't.

This is a cognitive problem.

Human reasoning naturally branches.

Example

"I'm planning a startup."

↓

Market

↓

Competition

↓

↓
Wait…

What about regulations?

↓

Actually…

What if I target students first?

↓

Back to pricing.

That's not distraction.

That's exploration.

Cognitive psychology often describes problem solving as involving exploration, hypothesis testing, and returning to earlier ideas rather than following a single straight line. You don't need to claim "the brain is literally a tree"; it's enough to observe that complex reasoning is iterative and branching in practice.

AI interfaces flatten that process into a timeline.
Notice the subtle difference.

We're not saying

Humans think like trees.

That's a bit absolute.

We're saying

Complex thinking naturally involves branching and revisiting ideas.

That's a claim we can support.

Then define the actual friction.

This is where I think your current document becomes stronger.

Instead of

"Context dilution"

I'd define several distinct UX problems.
Problem 1
Context Pollution

Every tangent becomes permanent context.

The main conversation accumulates unrelated questions that were only temporarily relevant.

Problem 2
Cognitive Navigation Cost

Returning to an earlier idea requires scrolling through irrelevant messages.

Users spend time navigating conversations instead of continuing their thinking.

Problem 3
Context Reset Penalty

To avoid polluting the current thread,

users often create a new chat.

But now they lose valuable upstream context.

They must reconstruct it manually.
Problem 4
Exploration Friction

Users become hesitant to ask

"small"

"temporary"

or

"what if"

questions

because they don't want to derail the conversation.

That subtly discourages exploration.

This one is my favorite.

It's deeper than clutter.
